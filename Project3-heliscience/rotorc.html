Overview

In this lesson we will look at different ways that the main rotors can be configured on a helicopter. We will cover the simplest of them all traditional and moving to the quad configuration.

Theory

Different main rotor configurations required different systems and mechanisms. But the principal is still the same. Every rotor that turns produces torque. If we don't cancel the torque produced by the working rotor blade the body of the helicopter would turn in the opposite direction. There area a few ways to cancel that torque.

First way is to introduce a tail rotor that is linked to the gearbox and works at the same speed as the main rotor and cancels it's torque, thus not making the helicopter spin around itself.
Second way is to have two or more rotors spinning in the opposite direction and canceling each other's torque.

Traditional rotor configuration relies on a tail rotor to cancel the torque created by the main rotor.
Tandem is when a helicopter has two rotor blades positioned one behind another, they spin in opposite direction and again cancel the each others torque.
Transverse is when rotor blades are positioned one next to another.
Coaxial, when both rotor blades are positioned on same mast one above another.
Intermeshing rotor blades sit on different masts, but as the name suggests their blades reach into other ones radius.

Conclusion

The point of this lesson is to learn the principals on which rotors produce lift and what are the consequences of doing so.
